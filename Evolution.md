# The Evolution of Rekvin: A Prototyping Journey

Rekvin has evolved through three distinct phases, shifting from a node-based workflow tool to an autonomous, persona-driven testing engine. This document captures the strategic pivots and design iterations that led to the current state of the platform.

---

## Phase 1: Contextual Node-Based Workflows (Rekvin v1)

The initial vision for Rekvin was to bridge the gap between creative ideation and technical feasibility by connecting design nodes into a coherent workflow. The goal was to provide designers with more context—linking "Killer Assumptions" and "Market Forces" directly to feature nodes.

> [!NOTE]
> While this provided better documentation, we realized the core output wasn't significantly different from traditional whiteboarding tools. It was a better filing system, but not a better design environment.

## Phase 2: Interconnected Canvases & Interface Comparison (Rekvin v2)

In the second iteration, we pivoted to a multi-canvas architecture. The breakthrough idea was that the **output node of one canvas could serve as the input for another**, allowing for a modular, non-linear design process. 

However, we soon discovered two things:
1. Managing different canvases added cognitive overhead that didn't necessarily improve the design quality.
2. The "cost of building" (time to prototype) decreased significantly, shifting the focus from *how* to build to *how* to compare multiple interface variations.

## Phase 3: Autonomous Persona Testing (Rekvin v3 - Current)

The current version of Rekvin represents our most significant shift. We realized that the most valuable "output" isn't the prototype itself, but the **validation** of that prototype. 

We now focus on deploying **autonomous synthetic personas** to test interfaces in real-time. By leveraging multimodal AI, we can extract high-fidelity insights, behavioral metrics, and "expert-driven verdicts" from automated testing sessions.

## Conclusion

Rekvin has moved from **contextual documentation** to **autonomous validation**. By focusing on how personas interact with interfaces rather than just how researchers draw them, we are building a tool that doesn't just sketch products—it stress-tests them.
